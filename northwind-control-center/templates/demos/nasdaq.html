NASDAQ operates a pure electronic continuous auction where buy and sell orders enter a central limit order book and match automatically at the best available price with no human market maker required. Price-time priority determines execution order: the highest bid wins, and when two bids match the same ask price, the earlier-arriving order executes first — to the microsecond. Unlike NYSE, NASDAQ has no Designated Market Maker obligation; liquidity comes entirely from competing algorithmic market makers who earn the bid-ask spread by quoting both sides simultaneously and withdrawing in volatility. The depth-of-book visualisation shows how large institutional orders create visible price walls that influence where other participants place their own orders.

NASDAQ is only a matching engine — it holds nothing. A Broker (Schwab, Robinhood) routes your order in via the internet. The engine matches it instantly, but legal ownership only transfers after T+1 settlement — the DTCC runs a batch process the next business day and the DTC Custodian updates the book-entry ledger. HFT firms pay millions for colocation (sub-millisecond latency) vs retail's ~50ms cloud path. Contrast the right panel: custodian holdings do not change until settlement fires.
