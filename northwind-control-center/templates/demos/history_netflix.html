Two Simultaneous Existential Threats

Threat 1: The Backbone Chokepoints
The internet is not a cloud — it's a physical network. Large ISPs (Tier-1 providers like AT&T, Comcast) connect to each other at "peering points" (IXPs). When millions of people tried to stream HD video simultaneously, these peering connections became saturated. Congestion at IXPs meant buffering, quality drops, and customer complaints. Netflix had no control over these physical bottlenecks.
Threat 2: ISP Throttling
Comcast, Verizon, and AT&T were spending hundreds of millions of dollars upgrading their backbone infrastructure — primarily because Netflix was using so much of it. They began throttling Netflix traffic and demanded Netflix pay "transit fees" to ensure delivery quality. Comcast openly throttled Netflix speeds from 2014 until a paid peering agreement was signed. This was the net neutrality crisis.
The AWS Limitation
Netflix ran on AWS. AWS has data centers in specific regions — not in every ISP's local exchange. Serving 4K video from AWS us-east-1 to a user in suburban Iowa meant: AWS data center → internet backbone → multiple ISP hops → user's house. Each hop adds latency and is subject to congestion. At peak (8 PM Friday), this path would saturate.
The Physics of Video
4K HDR video at high quality: ~25 Mbps sustained per stream. 1 million concurrent 4K streams = 25 Tbps. No single cloud region can handle this. No CDN with a reasonable number of PoPs can handle this economically. The data must be physically closer to viewers.
The Open Connect Trojan Horse
The Offer ISPs Couldn't Refuse
Netflix approached ISPs with a deal: "We'll give you free hardware. You rack it in your data center. We fill it with Netflix content. Your customers get a dramatically better Netflix experience. Your backbone load drops by 30-40%. Everyone wins."
Open Connect Appliance (OCA)
Custom server: 100–250 TB SSD storage, 100 Gbps network cards. Runs Netflix's custom Linux OS with a content delivery daemon. Physical size: 1U or 2U rack unit. Cost to Netflix: ~$5,000–15,000 per unit. Saves Netflix ~$1B/year in CDN fees vs using commercial CDNs.
Content Pre-positioning (2 AM Strategy)
Each night between 12AM-6AM (off-peak), Netflix's AWS origin servers push encrypted content to nearby Open Connect Appliances. The algorithm predicts: "What will viewers in this region want to watch tomorrow evening?" Popular new releases, trending shows, local content — pre-staged hours before demand peaks.
Cache Fill Algorithm
Not all content can be pre-staged (100 TB appliance, 40K+ titles). Netflix fills each OCA with: Top 200 shows in that region + all episodes of trending series + new releases. Long-tail content: served from nearby large OCAs or AWS origin on demand, cached locally after first request.

OCA Health System
Each OCA reports: content catalog (which titles are pre-staged), current utilization (Gbps out / 100 Gbps capacity), error rates. Steering service routes around overloaded or broken OCAs in real time. During a local ISP outage, traffic falls back to the nearest large "embedded" OCA cluster or AWS origin. No single OCA failure affects more than a small geographic area.
Two-Tier OCA Deployment: Embedded vs Exchange
Embedded OCAs (Inside ISP)
Placed directly inside ISP's own data centers. Netflix traffic never touches the internet peering points. Zero transit fees. Best performance: lowest latency, no congestion. Only accessible to that ISP's subscribers.
✓ Comcast, AT&T, Verizon, BT, Vodafone
✓ 1–3 hops from viewer's home
✓ 100 Gbps uplink per appliance
Exchange OCAs (At IXPs)
Larger clusters placed at major Internet Exchange Points (AMS-IX, DE-CIX, LINX, etc.). Accessible to many ISPs simultaneously. Handle long-tail content and geographic regions where Netflix hasn't placed embedded OCAs. Serve smaller ISPs cost-effectively.
⚡ Hundreds of ISPs served from one location
⚡ Higher bandwidth per unit (400 Gbps+)
⚡ Cross-continental fallback
The Economic Genius: Comcast was spending ~$500M/year upgrading backbone infrastructure, 30-40% of which was driven by Netflix traffic. Netflix spent ~$200M building Open Connect. Once OCAs were installed in Comcast's data centers, that 30-40% of backbone upgrade cost disappeared for Comcast. Netflix eliminated the transit problem by making ISPs willing partners instead of adversaries. The "net neutrality" fight essentially ended for Netflix once Open Connect was deployed — Netflix no longer needed the backbone at all.

The Lasting Verdict

Netflix Open Connect proved that for video streaming at internet scale, commercial CDNs are insufficient and cloud infrastructure alone is inadequate. The solution required going all the way to the physical network — placing custom hardware inside the buildings that house the last-mile connections to users' homes. This "hyper-local CDN" model is now considered best practice for any large-scale video platform. Google YouTube, Apple TV+, Disney+, and Amazon Prime Video all operate similar custom CDN infrastructure. The insight — that moving data physically closer to viewers is more valuable than any software optimization — became foundational to modern media delivery architecture.

💰 The Economics
Commercial CDN (Akamai): ~$0.005/GB. Netflix serves ~15 Tbps peak → 15,000 GB/s → $75/second → $6.5M/day in CDN fees. Open Connect amortized hardware cost: ~$0.0005/GB — 10× cheaper. Annually: saves Netflix $2B+. Total OCA hardware cost: ~$200M one-time investment. Payback period: under 5 weeks.
🎬 The Content Encryption Problem
Studios require DRM (Digital Rights Management) on all content. Netflix uses Widevine (Google) + PlayReady (Microsoft) + FairPlay (Apple) for different devices. Video files on OCAs are encrypted; the decryption key is issued by Netflix's license server (AWS) only to authenticated devices. Even if someone physically stole an OCA hard drive, the content is unreadable.
🌍 Who Copied It
  • YouTube — Google Global Cache (GGC), similar ISP placement
  • Apple — Custom CDN for App Store + Apple TV+
  • Meta — TPOP edge nodes at ISPs
  • Akamai — Evolved its model: now also places hardware inside ISPs
